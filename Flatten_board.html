How to flatten a board with just one plane

Tools required:

  • No. 5 jack plane
  • Workbench with vise and dog holes
  • Winding sticks
  • Straight edge
  • Pencil

Step 1: Set your plane

If you've never used a hand plane before, I'd recommend getting familiar with the basics by watching the video below:

To flatten rough stock, you'll want to start by taking a relatively thick shaving with your plane, otherwise this is going to take a while. You'll do this by backing your chip breaker a bit from the cutting edge of your plane iron. You don't want to go too far or you won't be able to push through the board. The distance from the cutting edge to your chipbreaker will determine the maximum thickness shaving you'll get in a pass. Take as thick of a shaving as you can comfortably take without tearing our your wood. A cambered iron will speed things up here, but it won't be smooth, so if you want to do this with just one plane, you'll have to make do.

Step 2: Assess the board

It's important to get an idea of how far you are from flat and in what way. Bowed and cupped are the most straight forward. Place a straight edge across your stock, and look for light below it. Those are your low spots. Mark your high spots with pencil; that's where you'll be removing material.

Twisting is a little trickier, and this is where you're winding sticks come in. The simplest way to see if your stock is twisted is by pressing down on opposite corners. If it rocks or wobbles, its twisted. The goal is still the same though, plane off the high spots until you're flat. To do this, put your winding sticks on the ends of your board and look down them.

The winding sticks exagerrate the twist to make it easier to see. Sight along the top and mark the high corners with pencil.

Step 3: Take a few passes

Take a few passes diagonally across the grain of your wood. You'll notice that you won't be taking a continuous shaving as your plane will only take off the high spots. Focus on those high corners if you're board is twisted first, but don't focus on any one spot for more than one pass; work your way all the way across your board. Check back with your winding sticks occasionally to measure your progress. As you take more passes, getting closer to a full length shaving, check your progress periodically. Use your straight edge or the side of your plane and look for light underneath. You won't see any when you're dead flat.

Step 4: Finish up

Once you're pretty close, set your plane up for a thinner shaving by moving that chipbreaker in close to the edge of your plane iron. Plane with the grain, taking overlapping passes with the grain of the wood. You should already be pretty close to flat, you're goal here is to remove any tool marks left from when you were hogging material off before. Don't take too many passes in one spot or on on side or your board won't be flat anymore. Make sure to remove material evenly from the whole surface.

Step 5: Congrats you're done!

Now that you've got one flat face, you can use it as a reference for squaring up your board.
